Replication: Karlan & List (2007)

Does Price Matter in Charitable Giving?

Author

Yiwei Zhang

Published

April 21, 2026

Introduction

Karlan and List (2007) study whether matching grants increase charitable giving in a large-scale natural field experiment. The authors partnered with a nonprofit organization and mailed donation solicitations to prior donors. Some donors were randomly assigned to a control group, while others received a matching offer. Within the treatment group, the authors also randomized the match ratio, the size threshold of the match, and the example donation amount. The paper asks whether lowering the effective “price” of giving through a matching grant changes donor behavior.

This is a causal research design because treatment was randomly assigned. If randomization worked, then treatment and control groups should look similar before the intervention, and any later differences in donations can be interpreted as causal effects of the matching offer.

def mean_se(series):
    mean = series.mean()
    se = series.std(ddof=1) / np.sqrt(series.notna().sum())
    return f"{mean:.3f} ({se:.3f})"

Something Additional

This paper supports a causal interpretation because treatment was randomly assigned. Since the treatment and control groups are balanced on observable characteristics, differences in donation outcomes after the mailing can be interpreted as causal effects of the matching offer rather than simple correlations.

An additional insight from the results is that offering a match appears to matter more than increasing the generosity of the match ratio itself. The results show that while the presence of a matching grant increases giving, larger match ratios (such as 2:1 or 3:1) do not generate much additional effect compared to a 1:1 match.
